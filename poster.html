Introduction

AI coding assistants like GitHub Copilot and ChatGPT are now standard tools in software development, but adoption hinges on how much developers trust the output these tools produce. Understanding what drives that trust or distrust is important for both tool designers and organizations rolling out AI-assisted workflows.

In this project we ask: what factors predict whether a professional developer trusts AI-generated output? We treat this as a classification problem and apply three independent methods, then compare their findings to validate the key drivers.

Data

We use the Stack Overflow Developer Survey from 2024 and 2025, combined into a single dataset. Both years ask identical core questions about AI tool usage and trust, making them directly compatible.

  • Combined dataset: ~87,600 responses → 55,118 professional developers with known AIAcc
  • After complete-case filtering: 37,840 developers used for modeling
  • Target variable (AIAcc): "How much do you trust the accuracy of AI tool output?" — 5-level ordinal from Highly trust to Highly distrust
  • Binary encoding: Somewhat/Highly distrust = 1 (distrust), everything else = 0

Key features used:

  • AI sentiment toward tools (AISent)
  • Perceived AI complexity handling (AIComplex)
  • Years of coding experience (YearsCode)
  • Age, education level, org size, remote work status, AI tool usage

Methodology

Bayesian Logistic Regression (R / brms)

  • Filtered to AI users only (AISelect == "Yes")
  • Binary target: distrust vs. non-distrust
  • Formula: y ~ YearsCode_z + AISent + AIThreat + AIComplex + year
  • Priors: student_t(4, 0, 10) intercept, normal(0, 2) coefficients
  • 4 chains, 1000 iterations — provides full posterior distributions and uncertainty estimates

User-User Collaborative Filtering (Python / NumPy)

  • Developer profiles encoded as feature vectors: manual z-score on numerics, one-hot on categoricals
  • Manual cosine similarity: SC(x,y) = xTy / (||x|| · ||y||)
  • Row-wise min-max scaling of similarity scores, top-20 neighbor weighted average prediction
  • Fully manual implementation using NumPy only — no sklearn
  • Predicts ordinal AIAcc score (0–4) as a continuous output

MLP Neural Network (Python / TensorFlow)

  • Grid search over 4 architectures × 2 learning rates
  • Best config: 64 hidden units, lr = 0.01, SGD, ReLU activations
  • Binary cross-entropy loss, sigmoid output, 50 epochs
  • 80/20 stratified train/test split

Discussion and Conclusion
Key Finding: Attitude toward AI, not developer demographics, is the primary driver of trust in AI output. All three models independently arrive at this conclusion.
  • AI sentiment is the dominant predictor. Developers with very unfavorable AI sentiment show a mean predicted trust score of 0.57 vs. 2.48 for very favorable — a nearly 2-point spread on a 4-point scale. Consistent across Bayes and CF.
  • Perceived AI competence matters. Developers who think AI handles complex tasks very poorly score 1.01 vs. 2.64 for those who think it handles them very well.
  • Experience negatively correlates with trust. Developers with 20+ years of experience average a predicted trust score of 1.70 compared to 2.13 for those with under 5 years.
  • CF limitation: Weighted average predictions compress toward the mean (1.5–2.5 range), reducing sensitivity to extreme classes. Binary accuracy remains competitive at 76.55%.
  • Cross-model consistency between Bayesian regression and CF on AISent and AIComplex strengthens confidence in these findings across methodologically independent approaches.
